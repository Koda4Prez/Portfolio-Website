＜/ARTIST STATEMENT.＞

There's something nostalgic about capturing moments and looking back at them a day, a week, or even a month later. Sometimes, without a camera in hand, I'll look between buildings down at the wet alleys on Seattle's streets, just a quick glance while walking by. I notice how the light reflects off of the puddles on the ground, how the rain is slowly dripping from the roofs of buildings, and how the person on the other side of the alley, oblivious to his contribution, brings the whole scene together. In that fraction of a second, I wish I could take the scene and stretch it on forever, filth and all. My photography is deeply rooted in that same space within everyday glances.

I often do street photography with some abstract art here and there. I am drawn to take snippets of my walks after class when I spot something interesting or the way the city feels when the sun is setting. The images I take are my way of documenting things that are often taken for granted.

For a great while, I didn't know what kind of photography I wanted to take, and it might be noticeable in the gallery. I moved between styles, tried framing subjects, and changed my way of looking at things. I just wanted to find something that felt authentic.

But recently I've been adapting the practice to capture whatever is in front of me at any given moment. I want to capture more of what I see without filtering anything out. I've found that by letting go of my own expectations, I've recovered the joy in photography that I lost when I wanted to become better at it.
